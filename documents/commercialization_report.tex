\begin{center}
    \Large
    \textbf{ABSTRACT}
\end{center}

\vspace{0.5cm}

\noindent
STYLMORPH is an innovative AI-powered virtual try-on platform that revolutionizes online fashion retail by enabling users to visualize garments on their own photographs and generate personalized 3D avatars from single 2D images. The system integrates a robust two-stage processing workflow combining high-speed 2D virtual try-on using the FASHN-VTON framework with detailed 3D avatar generation using PIFuHD monocular 3D reconstruction. By providing both immediate 2D garment previews and detailed 3D avatars for comprehensive 360-degree fitting analysis, Stylmorph significantly mitigates size and fit discrepancies. The MERN-stack architecture, augmented by Three.js WebGL rendering and Cloudinary CDN delivery, proves that high-fidelity fashion visualization is viable for real-world e-commerce deployment. This interactive and personalized shopping experience aims to reduce the high product return rates burdening the retail sector and substantially augment consumer confidence in online apparel purchases.

\textbf{Keywords:} Virtual Try-On, 3D Avatars, Monocular 3D Reconstruction, PIFuHD, FASHN-VTON, E-commerce, Computer Vision, Deep Learning, Three.js

\chapter{INTRODUCTION}

The rapid growth of e-commerce has fundamentally transformed how consumers shop for clothing, yet significant challenges persist in providing accurate fit visualization and personalized shopping experiences online. At least 30-40\% of online fashion purchases are returned due to improper fit and inability to visualize clothing on individual body types. Traditional e-commerce platforms rely primarily on static 2D images and generic size charts, leaving customers uncertain about how garments will fit their unique body proportions and personal style preferences. This uncertainty contributes to purchase hesitation, high return rates, and overall customer dissatisfaction.

In the rapidly evolving landscape of e-commerce and digital fashion, STYLMORPH emerges as a comprehensive virtual try-on platform designed to address the lack of personalization and fit visualization in modern e-commerce. Early attempts at virtual fitting rooms relied on users manually entering extensive body measurements to generate generic parametric bodies. While functional, these systems lacked visual personalization and real-world fidelity. To address these limitations, STYLMORPH introduces a two-stage approach that combines 2D visualization with detailed 3D avatar generation.

The system operates through a sophisticated pipeline that begins with user input collection. Users can upload a single full-body image, and the system first applies 2D virtual try-on using FASHN-VTON to synthesize selected garments on the user's photograph, preserving garment details, textures, and folds while adapting to the target person's pose and body shape. Subsequently, the same image is used to generate a personalized 3D avatar using PIFuHD, a multi-level pixel-aligned implicit function that enables high-resolution 3D human digitization from a single image, preserving details such as clothing folds and hair.

STYLMORPH's architecture comprises multiple interconnected modules built on the MERN stack (MongoDB, Express.js, React.js, Node.js) with specialized Python microservices for machine learning processing. The platform combines an intuitive React.js frontend with an AI-driven backend. Due to infrastructure constraints, the FASHN-VTON 2D try-on pipeline is deployed on Google Colaboratory with a GPU runtime, accessed via secure API tunneling from the Node.js backend. The PIFuHD pipeline runs locally within a Python subprocess. Generated meshes are delivered rapidly via Cloudinary and rendered in the browser using Three.js, enabling hardware-accelerated, real-time interactive visualization of 3D avatars.

This comprehensive approach not only enhances user confidence in purchasing decisions but also reduces the environmental impact of modern retail by minimizing product returns and associated logistics. By accurately simulating how clothing items appear on individual body types, STYLMORPH significantly enhances customer confidence in online purchases, reduces return rates, and creates a more engaging shopping experience.

\chapter{PROBLEM STATEMENT}

The online fashion industry faces significant challenges with high return rates primarily due to improper fit and inability to visualize clothing on individual body types. Traditional e-commerce platforms provide limited tools for customers to assess how garments will look on their specific body shape, leading to purchase uncertainty and dissatisfaction. 

Current virtual try-on solutions often rely on generic parametric bodies like the SMPL model, which typically produces naked or minimally-clothed bodies stripped of hair and complex textures. These standardized representations fail to capture the nuanced details that consumers need to make confident purchasing decisions.

\section{Objective}

The key objectives of Stylmorph are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item To provide instant 2D try-on results for rapid garment browsing using FASHN-VTON
    \item To generate detailed, topologically accurate 3D avatars from a single photo without specialized hardware using PIFuHD
    \item To enable real-time, 360-degree interactive inspection of how garments fit the user's body within a standard web browser
    \item To create a scalable web architecture with MERN stack for seamless user experience
    \item To reduce return rates through accurate fit prediction and realistic visualization
\end{itemize}

\chapter{EXISTING SYSTEM}

Current virtual try-on solutions in the e-commerce landscape suffer from several limitations that restrict their effectiveness and adoption. Most existing systems rely on standardized body models that fail to account for individual variations in body shape, proportions, and measurements. These generic avatars provide inaccurate representations of how clothing will actually fit and appear on specific users.

\section{Current Virtual Try-On Solutions}

Existing virtual try-on platforms typically use one-size-fits-all approaches that cannot accurately simulate clothing fit across diverse body types. Research on virtual try-on systems has evolved significantly over the past decade, but early solutions primarily used 2D image overlays and basic body measurement calculators. While these systems provided basic functionality, they struggled with realistic volume simulation and true personalized fit accuracy.

The introduction of sophisticated 3D modeling and monocular reconstruction techniques marked a massive leap forward. Loper et al. introduced the Skinned Multi-Person Linear (SMPL) model, a vertex-based model that represents a wide variety of human body shapes accurately. However, SMPL typically produces naked or minimally-clothed bodies stripped of hair and complex textures, limiting its utility for realistic virtual try-on.

\section{Disadvantages of Existing System}

Current systems face the following challenges:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Generic Avatar Models:} Use standardized body types that don't reflect individual user measurements, failing to capture detailed features like clothing wrinkles and hairstyles
    \item \textbf{Limited Customization:} Offer minimal options for body shape adjustments and rely on parametric constraints
    \item \textbf{Inaccurate Fit Prediction:} Cannot accurately simulate how different fabrics and cuts will drape on various body types
    \item \textbf{Poor Integration:} Limited connectivity with e-commerce platforms and often remain isolated research prototypes lacking seamless web deployment
    \item \textbf{Basic Visualization:} Provide 2D or simplified 3D representations that lack realism
    \item \textbf{Single-Modality Approaches:} Most systems offer either 2D try-on or 3D avatars, but rarely both in an integrated workflow
\end{itemize}

\chapter{LITERATURE SURVEY}

The field of 3D avatar generation and virtual try-on systems has witnessed significant advancement in recent years, driven by breakthroughs in computer vision, deep learning, and 3D reconstruction techniques. This chapter presents a comprehensive review of existing literature and research works relevant to the development of STYLMORPH, an AI-powered virtual try-on platform. The survey covers ten significant contributions that form the foundation for our work, ranging from parametric body models to neural rendering techniques.

\section*{1. PIFuHD: Multi-Level Pixel-Aligned Implicit Function for High-Resolution 3D Human Digitization (2020)}

\textbf{Authors: Shunsuke Saito, Zeng Huang, Ryota Natsume, Shigeo Morishima, Angjoo Kanazawa, Michael J. Black}

PIFuHD introduces a multi-level pixel-aligned implicit function that dramatically improved the resolution of 3D human digitization from a single image. The architecture employs a coarse-level network that observes the whole image for global context, and a fine-level network that focuses on local features to predict detailed surface geometry. This hierarchical approach enables the reconstruction of intricate details such as clothing wrinkles and hairstyles, generating realistic meshes without parametric constraints. The primary advantage of this method is its ability to produce high-resolution output with fine detail preservation, capturing elements like wrinkles and hair without being constrained by parametric models. It also works with a single image input, eliminating the need for multi-camera setups. However, the approach is computationally intensive, requiring significant processing time and a high-end GPU for inference. The quality of the reconstruction relies heavily on the input image's lighting, pose, and occlusions, and the model has a large memory footprint for high-resolution outputs. Furthermore, its complex training pipeline requires substantial data.

\section*{2. FASHN-VTON: High-Fidelity Virtual Try-On with Texture Preservation (2024)}

\textbf{Authors: FASHN Research Team}

FASHN-VTON represents a novel approach for high-fidelity virtual clothing transfer that preserves garment details, textures, and folds while adapting to the target person's pose and body shape. This framework builds upon earlier work in 2D virtual try-on such as VITON and CP-VTON, establishing a robust foundation for image-based clothing transfer with superior texture preservation and garment morphing capabilities. Its key advantages are high-fidelity texture preservation that maintains garment logos and patterns, accurate garment morphing that adapts to the target person's pose, and its ability to adapt to varied body shapes and poses effectively. It also preserves fine details like folds and fabric textures. On the downside, the method is computationally intensive, requiring GPU acceleration, and remote execution can introduce significant latency of 7-8 minutes. It requires high-quality input images for optimal results and is limited to 2D visualization without any depth information, with a dependency on external compute resources.

\section*{3. AG3D: Learning to Generate 3D Avatars from 2D Images (2023)}

\textbf{Authors: Zijian Dong, Xu Chen, Jinlong Yang, Michael J. Black, Otmar Hilliges, Andreas Geiger}

AG3D introduces a method for generating detailed 3D avatars from 2D images via Fast-SNARF, utilizing tri-plane features for detailed geometry reconstruction. The approach employs adversarial training to enhance realism and enables the generation of avatars without requiring 3D scans for training. Its main advantages are that it requires no 3D scans for training, working directly with 2D image datasets, while producing realistic avatars with detailed surface geometry and capturing clothing details effectively. Fast-SNARF enables efficient deformation handling, and tri-plane features provide an efficient geometry representation. However, it needs a high-end GPU for inference and is not suitable for real-time applications. The complex training pipeline requires expertise, and the results are limited by the quality of the input images. Additionally, the adversarial training process can be unstable.

\section*{4. CAPE: Clothed Auto-Personalized Avatars (2022)}

\textbf{Authors: Qianli Ma, Jinlong Yang, Anurag Ranjan, Sergi Pujades, Gerard Pons-Moll, Michael J. Black, Siyu Tang}

CAPE extends the SMPL model with a generative clothing layer, using a VAE-GAN architecture for realistic cloth deformation. The model provides pose-dependent garment adaptability, enabling realistic wrinkles and folds that respond to body movements. The system excels at generating realistic wrinkles and folds and works seamlessly with the standard SMPL model. It adapts to body poses with dynamic cloth deformation, and its generative approach enables varied clothing styles with pose-dependent modeling that enhances realism. The main disadvantages include its requirement for high-quality 3D scans for training and its limitation to garment types seen during training. Data collection and processing are expensive, and the inference is computationally intensive. Furthermore, it requires careful balancing of GAN training.

\section*{5. SMPL: A Skinned Multi-Person Linear Model (2015)}

\textbf{Authors: Matthew Loper, Naureen Mahmood, Javier Romero, Gerard Pons-Moll, Michael J. Black}

SMPL introduces a parametric human body model for animation, utilizing linear blend skinning and shape parameters to provide realistic body deformations. The model has become a widely adopted standard in computer vision and graphics research, serving as the foundation for numerous subsequent works. Its advantages include being a highly efficient parametric representation that integrates easily with animation pipelines. It has seen wide adoption in the research community, offers realistic body deformations with pose changes, and provides a compact representation through shape and pose parameters. The disadvantages are that it has limited clothing and hair detail, typically producing naked or minimally-clothed bodies. It requires parameter tuning for accuracy and lacks fine surface details and textures, making it unable to capture clothing wrinkles or hairstyles.

\section*{6. PIXIE: Portrait Image to Full-Body Avatar (2021)}

\textbf{Authors: Timo Bolkart, Jinlong Yang, Michael J. Black}

PIXIE generates full-body 3D models from single portrait images using deep shape and pose priors, combining facial and body reconstruction networks. The approach enables fast inference while capturing realistic body shapes from minimal input. It requires only a single image input, has a fast inference time suitable for applications, captures realistic body shape variations, combines facial and body reconstruction, and uses deep priors to ensure plausible shape generation. However, it is less accurate for occluded body parts and offers limited geometric detail for clothing. It is trained on specific dataset distributions and can struggle with extreme poses, and it may not capture fine surface details.

\section*{7. MetaHuman Creator: Ultra-Realistic Digital Humans (2021)}

\textbf{Authors: Epic Games Research Team}

MetaHuman Creator is a cloud-based system for creating ultra-realistic digital humans, offering real-time customization and facial rigging with direct integration into Unreal Engine. The platform represents the state-of-the-art in high-fidelity digital human creation. It achieves photorealistic human quality, offers easy customization through an intuitive interface, and provides real-time facial rigging and animation. It also offers cloud-based storage and processing with direct integration into game engines. The disadvantages are its heavy computational cost for rendering, its requirement of the Unreal Engine ecosystem, and its limited offline usage capabilities. It is not designed for single-photo reconstruction and is considered overkill for e-commerce try-on applications.

\section*{8. HumanNeRF: Neural Rendering for Dynamic Humans (2021)}

\textbf{Authors: Chung-Yi Wang, Tomas Simon, et al.}

HumanNeRF employs neural radiance fields for human reconstruction, handling motion with pose-conditioned NeRFs to produce high-fidelity dynamic humans. The approach enables photorealistic novel view synthesis of moving subjects. The method provides high visual realism with neural rendering, handles dynamic motion with pose-conditioning, produces high-fidelity dynamic human models, enables photorealistic novel view synthesis, and captures fine details like clothing folds. On the other hand, it has a slow rendering speed unsuitable for real-time, requires a complex neural network setup, is memory intensive for storage and processing, and its training requires multi-view video, as it is not designed for single-image inference.

\section*{9. Deep Fashion3D: Dataset for 3D Garment Reconstruction (2021)}

\textbf{Authors: Heming Zhu, Yu Cao, et al.}

Deep Fashion3D provides a large-scale dataset and benchmark for 3D garment reconstruction, including topology-consistent meshes specifically focused on fashion and virtual try-on applications. The dataset enables research in garment transfer and texture learning. It offers a large-scale dataset for clothing research with a topology-consistent mesh representation. It is specifically designed for fashion applications, supports texture learning and garment transfer, and serves as a benchmark for 3D garment reconstruction tasks. Its limitations include limited pose and garment variety, a requirement for large storage capacity, restricted license terms for commercial use, static poses that limit dynamic applications, and varying annotation quality across samples.

\section*{10. AvatarCLIP: Zero-Shot Text-Guided Avatar Generation (2022)}

\textbf{Authors: Fangzhou Hong, Mingyuan Zhang, et al.}

AvatarCLIP uses CLIP to align text with avatar shapes, generating avatars based on textual prompts through zero-shot learning for open vocabulary. The approach enables creative avatar design without dataset-specific training. Its advantages are that it requires no dataset-specific training, its text-guided generation enables creative design, its zero-shot learning works with an open vocabulary, it supports novel avatar concepts, and it is flexible and adaptable to user prompts. The disadvantages are that it offers limited fine control over avatar details, has lower geometric accuracy than specialized models, its output quality depends on the CLIP model, it may produce inconsistent results, and it cannot capture a specific user's likeness.

\section*{11. DreamHuman: Diffusion-Based Human Modeling (2023)}

\textbf{Authors: Jie Hu, et al.}

DreamHuman employs diffusion models for 3D human synthesis, combining multi-view diffusion and NeRFs to generate diverse realistic humans. The approach demonstrates strong generalization capabilities by integrating multiple state-of-the-art AI models. The strengths of DreamHuman are its high realism and diversity in generated humans, strong generalization across different human types, effective combination of multi-view diffusion with NeRFs, generation of diverse realistic human models, and successful integration of multiple AI models. The drawbacks are its very high computational cost, slow inference speed that limits applications, complex implementation requiring expertise, lack of optimization for single-image reconstruction, and the significant resources needed for training.

\section{Research Gap Identification}

Based on the comprehensive literature survey, the following research gaps have been identified:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Integrated 2D-3D Workflow:} Most existing systems focus either on 2D virtual try-on (FASHN-VTON, VITON, CP-VTON) or 3D avatar generation (PIFuHD, AG3D, PIXIE), but rarely provide both modalities in an integrated workflow accessible via standard web browsers.

    \item \textbf{Accessible Deployment:} State-of-the-art methods like HumanNeRF, DreamHuman, and MetaHuman require significant computational resources and specialized software ecosystems, limiting their deployment in consumer-facing web applications.

    \item \textbf{Single-Image Reconstruction with Detail Preservation:} While methods like PIFuHD and PIXIE work with single images, they often struggle with complete texture preservation or require parametric constraints that limit clothing fidelity.

    \item \textbf{Parallel Processing Architecture:} Existing systems typically process 2D and 3D modalities sequentially, forcing users to wait for complete pipeline execution before receiving any feedback.

    \item \textbf{Real-time Web Rendering:} The challenge of streaming complex 3D meshes to browsers and rendering them interactively with WebGL remains underexplored in academic literature.

    \item \textbf{Texture Mapping Integration:} No existing system effectively projects 2D garment textures from virtual try-on onto reconstructed 3D meshes for photorealistic visualization.
\end{itemize}

STYLMORPH addresses these gaps by combining FASHN-VTON for high-fidelity 2D try-on with PIFuHD for detailed 3D reconstruction, implementing a parallel pipeline architecture that minimizes perceived wait time, and delivering both modalities through a scalable MERN stack architecture with Three.js-based WebGL rendering.

\chapter{PROPOSED SYSTEM}

In the proposed system, STYLMORPH employs a bifurcated parallel processing pipeline designed to balance speed and depth of visualization. Upon uploading a full-body image, the system simultaneously dispatches data to two distinct pipelines. The primary pipeline ports the image to a remote Google Colaboratory environment via a secure tunneling connection, where the FASHN-VTON framework performs garment warping and synthesis to produce a photorealistic 2D try-on result. The secondary pipeline runs PIFuHD locally within a Python subprocess to generate a dense 3D 'obj' mesh encoding the user's full body topology.

\section{Dataset}
The STYLMORPH framework leverages a combination of widely-recognized benchmarks and user-contributed datasets to ensure robust performance across various body types and garment styles. For the 2D Virtual Try-On module, we reference datasets like VITON-HD, while the 3D reconstruction pipeline utilizes pre-trained PIFuHD checkpoints. The application dynamically processes user-uploaded high-resolution images, which are stored in Cloudinary's cloud infrastructure. This real-time dataset allows users to see personalized results based on their own physical attributes, moving beyond static libraries.

\subsection{Algorithms Used}

\subsubsection{PIFuHD Algorithm (3D Model Generation)}
The PIFuHD (Pixel-Aligned Implicit Function for High-Resolution) algorithm is used to generate a detailed 3D human model from a single 2D image. It learns an implicit representation of the human body to achieve high-resolution reconstruction.

Initially, a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) extracts important features from the input image, such as body shape, clothing details, and textures. For every point in the 3D space, the algorithm predicts whether the point lies inside or outside the human body using an implicit function.

To maintain spatial consistency, each 3D point is projected back onto the 2D image plane, ensuring alignment with the original image pixels. After evaluating all points, the Marching Cubes algorithm is applied to convert the implicit representation into a continuous 3D surface.

The final output is a high-resolution \texttt{.obj} mesh that captures fine details such as wrinkles, hair, and body structure.



\subsubsection{FASHN-VTON Algorithm (2D Virtual Try-On)}
The FASHN-VTON algorithm is used to generate realistic 2D virtual try-on results by digitally applying garments onto a user’s image. It is based on a diffusion model, specifically a Multimodal Diffusion Transformer (MMDiT), which progressively transforms noise into a realistic dressed image.

To ensure proper garment fitting, Thin Plate Spline (TPS) transformation is used to warp the clothing image according to the user’s body shape and pose. Additionally, pose estimation techniques such as DWPose detect key body landmarks (e.g., shoulders, arms, and waist), enabling accurate alignment of garments.

// avatar.js - Controller for 2D VTON
exports.viewWearable = async (req, res) => {
    const avatar = await Avatar.findOne({ userId: req.user.id });
    const wearable = avatar.wearables.find(w => w._id.toString() === wearableId);
    
    // Convert images to Base64 for processing server
    const personBase64 = await downloadAsBase64(avatar.imageUrl);
    const garmentBase64 = await downloadAsBase64(wearable.url);

    // Call external GPU endpoint (VTON-HD/FASHN based)
    const tryonResponse = await fetch("https://vton-api-tunnel.ngrok-free.dev/tryon", {
      method: "POST",
      body: JSON.stringify({
        person_image: personBase64,
        garment_image: garmentBase64,
        category: "tops"
      }),
    });

    const tryonData = await tryonResponse.json();
    const resultUrl = await uploadToCloudinary(tryonData.result_image);
    
    avatar.tryonResults.push({ url: resultUrl, category: "tops" });
    await avatar.save();
};
\end{verbatim}

\chapter{SOFTWARE TESTING}

Software testing is a process used for verifying the correctness, completeness and quality of the developed software. Software is built out of sub-systems that are composed of modules, which in turn are composed of procedures and functions. The sequence of testing activities performed for the STYLMORPH system is as below:

\begin{itemize}
    \item Unit Testing
    \item Integration Testing
    \item System Testing
    \item Functional Testing
\end{itemize}

\section{Unit Testing}

There exist a number of components in every sub-system. Every component is tested using respective test procedures. Each component is tested individually based on their needs. Unit test focuses verification effort on the smallest unit of the software design component. Each module can be tested using the following two Strategies:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Black Box Testing:} In this strategy some test cases are generated as input conditions that fully execute all functional requirements for the program.
    \item \textbf{White Box Testing:} In this the test cases are generated on the logic of each module by drawing flow graphs of that module and logical decisions are tested on all the cases.
\end{enumerate}

\section{System Testing}

System testing is the stage of implementation. Testing is the process of executing a program with the intent of finding an error. A good test case is one that has a high probability of finding a yet undiscovered error.

System testing of software or hardware is testing conducted on a complete, integrated system to evaluate the system's compliance with its specified requirements. System testing falls within the scope of black-box testing, and as such, should require no knowledge of the inner design of the code or logic.

\section{Future Directions}

Future work will focus on three key areas:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Migrating FASHN-VTON Pipeline:} Move the FASHN-VTON pipeline to a dedicated GPU inference server to reduce latency from minutes to seconds
    \item \textbf{Texture Mapping Integration:} Project FASHN-VTON 2D texture maps directly onto PIFuHD 3D mesh surfaces to produce fully textured photorealistic avatars
    \item \textbf{Physics-Based Cloth Simulation:} Implement algorithms within the Three.js ecosystem to calculate collision layers between generated human meshes and digital garments, ensuring realistic fabric drape and avoiding geometric clipping
    \item \textbf{Real-time Computer Vision Pre-processor:} Integrate client-side photo quality validation to guide users in taking optimal setup photos
    \item \textbf{Augmented Reality Integration:} Enable real-time virtual try-on using smartphone cameras
    \item \textbf{Sustainability Features:} Show environmental impact of fashion choices by visualizing reduced return rates and carbon footprint
\end{itemize}

The success of STYLMORPH demonstrates the growing importance of virtual try-on technology in the future of retail. As consumer expectations evolve and technology advances, systems like STYLMORPH will play an increasingly crucial role in shaping how people discover, try, and purchase fashion items. The foundation established by this project provides a solid platform for continued innovation in personalized digital fashion experiences.
